## 1. Audit Your Current Website Performance

New Zealand's internet infrastructure has improved dramatically with fibre rollout, but performance expectations vary across the country. Urban Auckland and Wellington have excellent connectivity; rural areas can still face challenges.

Key metrics to audit:

- **Page speed from NZ servers** — Test from Auckland and Wellington-based servers, not just US or Australian ones. Hosting location matters for NZ visitors.
- **Mobile traffic share** — Approximately 78% of NZ web traffic is mobile, with high smartphone penetration across demographics
- **Traffic by city** — Auckland dominates (it's a third of the population), but Wellington, Christchurch, Hamilton, and Tauranga are distinct markets
- **Search performance** — Track both google.co.nz and google.com.au performance if you serve both markets
- **Conversion funnels** — Map the complete user journey; Kiwi users value clear, honest communication and won't tolerate hard-sell tactics
- **Trans-Tasman traffic** — If you serve Australian clients, track that traffic separately

Establish clear baselines. New Zealand business culture values practical results — you need data to prove the redesign was worth the investment.

## 4. Align with NZ Accessibility Standards

New Zealand's Web Accessibility Standard (based on WCAG 2.1) is mandatory for government agencies and strongly recommended for all businesses. The NZ Government Web Standards require Level AA compliance for all government web content.

If you serve government clients or aspire to public sector contracts, WCAG 2.1 AA compliance is effectively mandatory. For all other NZ businesses, it's increasingly expected.

Accessibility checklist:

- **WCAG 2.1 Level AA** across all pages
- **Colour contrast** meeting minimum ratios
- **Keyboard navigation** for all interactive elements
- **Screen reader compatibility** with proper semantic HTML
- **Alt text** on all meaningful images
- **Form accessibility** — clear labels, error handling, and instructions
- **Focus indicators** visible on all interactive elements
- **Accessibility statement** — not legally required for private sector but increasingly expected

Webflow's clean semantic HTML provides a strong foundation for accessibility compliance. Unlike WordPress, where accessibility depends on theme quality and plugin choices, Webflow gives you direct control over the HTML structure and ARIA attributes.

## 5. Consider Te Reo Maori and Bicultural Design

New Zealand's bicultural identity is increasingly reflected in digital spaces. Te reo Maori is an official language of New Zealand, and its inclusion in websites signals cultural competence and respect.

Content decisions:

- **Minimum:** Use te reo Maori greetings and key terms naturally (e.g., "Nau mai, haere mai" as a welcome, "Aotearoa" alongside "New Zealand")
- **Moderate:** Bilingual key pages (home, about, contact) with te reo Maori alongside English
- **Full:** Comprehensive te reo Maori content for organizations serving Maori communities or government

Design considerations:

- **Maori design elements** — If incorporating Maori visual motifs (kowhaiwhai, tukutuku patterns), engage with Maori designers or cultural advisors. Cultural appropriation is a serious concern — these elements must be used respectfully and appropriately.
- **Place names** — Use Maori place names alongside English where appropriate (Tamaki Makaurau/Auckland, Te Whanganui-a-Tara/Wellington, Otautahi/Christchurch)
- **Tikanga** — Your website's tone should reflect NZ values of manaakitanga (hospitality), whanaungatanga (relationship building), and kaitiakitanga (stewardship/sustainability)

This isn't just cultural sensitivity — it's good business. NZ government, iwi organizations, and many enterprises increasingly expect digital partners to demonstrate bicultural competence.

## 6. Comply with the NZ Privacy Act 2020

New Zealand's Privacy Act 2020 governs how businesses collect, store, and use personal information. The Office of the Privacy Commissioner (OPC) enforces compliance.

Key requirements for your website:

- **Privacy statement** — Must be accessible and clearly explain what information you collect, why, and how it's used
- **Collection purpose** — Personal information can only be collected for a lawful purpose connected to your business
- **Consent** — Clear, informed consent for data collection
- **Cookie disclosure** — While NZ privacy law doesn't specifically regulate cookies like GDPR, best practice is transparent disclosure
- **Overseas disclosure** — If personal data is transferred outside NZ (e.g., to US-based analytics tools), you must take reasonable steps to ensure comparable protection
- **Data breach notification** — Mandatory notification to the OPC for privacy breaches that pose a risk of harm

Build privacy compliance into the redesign from the start. The Privacy Commissioner has been increasingly active in guidance and enforcement.

## 8. Design Mobile-First for NZ

With 78% of NZ web traffic on mobile, mobile-first is the only sensible approach.

NZ-specific mobile considerations:

- **Variable connectivity** — Urban NZ has excellent mobile coverage (Spark, One NZ, 2degrees), but rural areas — which include many tourism and primary industry businesses — can have limited connectivity. Design for graceful degradation on slower connections.
- **Practical, no-nonsense design** — Kiwi users respond to clean, honest design. Over-designed, flashy websites feel inauthentic.
- **Click-to-call prominence** — NZ business culture still values phone calls; make phone contact easy on mobile
- **Maps integration** — Important for tourism, hospitality, and service businesses; ensure Google Maps integrates well on mobile
- **Touch targets** — 44px minimum for all interactive elements
- **Minimal form fields** — 3-5 fields maximum on mobile. Kiwi users won't fill out lengthy forms on their phones.

Test on iPhone (Safari) and Android (Chrome). NZ's smartphone market is approximately 55/45 iPhone/Android.

## 9. Plan Content Migration

Content migration for NZ businesses requires attention to local details and cultural accuracy.

Migration process:

1. **Inventory all content** — pages, blog posts, case studies, resources
2. **Evaluate each piece** — traffic, conversions, SEO value, relevance
3. **Categorize:** Keep, Rewrite, Consolidate, Remove
4. **Map URLs** — old to new for every page
5. **Update NZ-specific content:**
   - NZBN (NZ Business Number) and company details
   - Prices in NZD with correct formatting
   - GST references (15% in NZ)
   - Industry certifications and accreditations
   - Regulatory references (specific to NZ, not Australian or generic)
   - Seasonal references (NZ seasons are opposite to Northern Hemisphere — verify any seasonal content)
6. **Check cultural accuracy** — Te reo Maori usage, place names, cultural references should be correct and respectful

## 10. Optimize for NZ Local SEO

NZ's concentrated population creates intense local SEO competition in key cities.

Local SEO priorities:

- **Google Business Profile** — Accurate, complete listing for each location
- **City-specific pages** — Auckland (Tamaki Makaurau), Wellington (Te Whanganui-a-Tara), Christchurch (Otautahi), Hamilton (Kirikiriroa), Tauranga, Dunedin (Otepoti) as relevant
- **NZ keyword research** — Kiwi English has distinct vocabulary and spelling. Use NZ English (not American or British) consistently — "organisation" not "organization," "harbour" not "harbor."
- **Local schema markup** — LocalBusiness schema with NZ address formatting (suburb, city, postcode)
- **NZ directories** — Yellow Pages NZ, Finda, Localist, and industry-specific directories
- **NZ backlinks** — Links from NZ media (NZ Herald, Stuff, Business Desk), industry associations, and government sites (.govt.nz) carry strong local weight
- **Trans-Tasman SEO** — If serving Australian clients, optimize for google.com.au as well

## 11. Implement 301 Redirects

Every changed URL needs a redirect. This is how you protect the search authority your current site has built.

Steps:

1. Crawl your complete current site
2. Map every old URL to its new equivalent
3. Prioritize pages with backlinks from NZ media and business directories
4. Implement before launch
5. Verify with post-launch crawl

For [WordPress to Webflow migrations](/services/wordpress-migration/), URL structures typically change. WordPress's verbose URL patterns need clean mapping to Webflow's leaner structure.

## 14. Plan Your Launch

NZ business has its own calendar and rhythm. Plan accordingly.

Timing:

- **Ideal:** Tuesday or Wednesday, 09:00-10:00 NZST
- **Avoid:** Christmas-January period (NZ's summer holiday — many businesses slow dramatically from mid-December through January), Waitangi Day (February 6), Anzac Day (April 25), Matariki (June/July), school holidays
- **Best periods:** February-March (post-summer, everyone's back), August-November (post-winter, pre-holiday wind-down)

Launch day:

- DNS propagation monitoring
- SSL verification
- Privacy statement published and accessible
- Team notification
- LinkedIn announcement (LinkedIn is NZ's primary B2B platform)
- Instagram for consumer-facing businesses
- Monitor analytics throughout launch day

### What privacy requirements apply to NZ business websites?

The NZ Privacy Act 2020 requires businesses to have a clear privacy statement, collect personal information only for lawful purposes, obtain informed consent, and report data breaches to the Office of the Privacy Commissioner if they risk harm. ### How do I ensure my NZ website redesign doesn't hurt search rankings?

Three critical steps: First, crawl your entire current site and create a comprehensive 301 redirect map before launch — every old URL must redirect to its new equivalent. Second, maintain content quality and depth; don't delete high-performing content without redirecting it. Third, monitor Google Search Console daily for the first 30 days after launch to catch crawl errors immediately. NZ businesses that follow this process typically see rankings stabilize within 4-6 weeks, with improvements following as the new site's better technical performance benefits SEO.
