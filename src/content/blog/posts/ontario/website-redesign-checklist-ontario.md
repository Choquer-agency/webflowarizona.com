**A complete website redesign checklist for Ontario businesses covers 15 critical steps: auditing current site performance, defining business goals, researching your Toronto or GTA competitors, ensuring AODA accessibility compliance, planning bilingual content if needed, selecting a modern platform like Webflow, designing mobile-first, optimizing for local SEO, migrating content without losing rankings, and launching with a structured QA process. Ontario businesses that follow this checklist reduce redesign timelines by 40% and avoid the costly mistakes that plague rushed launches.**

If you're running a business in Ontario — whether you're a fintech startup in the MaRS Discovery District, a manufacturing company in Kitchener-Waterloo, or a professional services firm on Bay Street — your website is the first impression for the vast majority of your potential clients. And if that website was built more than three years ago, it's almost certainly holding you back.

Ontario's digital economy is the largest in Canada, with Toronto alone housing over 17,000 tech companies. The competition for attention online is fierce, and a dated website sends a clear message: this company isn't keeping up. But a redesign isn't something you rush into. Without a proper plan, you risk losing search rankings, breaking user flows, and wasting tens of thousands of dollars.

This checklist will walk you through every step, tailored specifically for businesses operating in Ontario's unique market.

## 1. Audit Your Current Website Performance

Before you change anything, you need a clear picture of what's working and what isn't. Pull your Google Analytics data for the past 12 months and identify your top-performing pages, highest-converting landing pages, and biggest traffic sources.

For Ontario businesses, pay special attention to:

- **Local search performance** — Are you ranking for "[service] + Toronto" or "[service] + Ontario" queries?
- **Mobile traffic share** — Ontario's mobile usage rate is above the national average at roughly 72% of web traffic
- **Page speed scores** — Google's Core Web Vitals are non-negotiable; run every key page through PageSpeed Insights
- **Bounce rates by region** — Check if visitors from the GTA behave differently than those from Ottawa or Hamilton

Document everything. Your current site's analytics are the baseline against which you'll measure the redesign's success. Too many Ontario businesses skip this step, launch a beautiful new site, and then can't prove whether it actually performs better.

## 2. Define Clear Business Objectives

A redesign without defined goals is just an expensive makeover. Before touching a single pixel, answer these questions:

- What specific business outcomes should the new site drive? (Lead generation, e-commerce sales, brand positioning)
- What's your target conversion rate improvement?
- Are you expanding into new Ontario markets (e.g., moving from Toronto-only to serving all of Southern Ontario)?
- Do you need to support both English and French content for government contracts or broader Canadian reach?

Ontario's economy is remarkably diverse. A Shopify app company in Waterloo has completely different website goals than an immigration law firm in Mississauga or a tourism operator in Muskoka. Your redesign objectives should reflect your specific business model and market position within the province.

## 3. Research Your Ontario Competitors

Pull up the websites of your top five competitors in Ontario. Not just the ones you know — use search to find who's actually ranking for your target keywords.

For each competitor, document:

- Their site structure and navigation approach
- Page load speeds (use GTmetrix or Google PageSpeed)
- Content depth and quality
- Call-to-action placement and conversion paths
- Mobile experience quality
- Whether they're using modern platforms like [Webflow](/) or still stuck on legacy WordPress setups

Toronto and the GTA specifically have seen a surge in businesses upgrading to modern web platforms over the past two years. If your competitors have already made the switch and you're still running a 2019 WordPress theme, you're at a measurable disadvantage in both user experience and search performance.

## 4. Ensure AODA Accessibility Compliance

This isn't optional for Ontario businesses. The Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act (AODA) requires that websites meet WCAG 2.0 Level AA standards. As of 2025, enforcement has increased, and non-compliant businesses face fines of up to $100,000 per day for corporations.

Your redesign checklist must include:

- **Colour contrast ratios** meeting WCAG AA minimums (4.5:1 for normal text)
- **Keyboard navigation** for all interactive elements
- **Alt text** on every meaningful image
- **Proper heading hierarchy** (H1 → H2 → H3, no skipping)
- **Form labels** properly associated with inputs
- **ARIA landmarks** for screen reader navigation
- **Focus indicators** visible on all interactive elements

## 5. Plan Your Bilingual Content Strategy

Ontario is officially English-speaking, but if you serve clients across Canada — especially government contracts — bilingual capability matters. Even within Ontario, the Franco-Ontarian community numbers over 620,000 people, concentrated in Ottawa, Sudbury, and along the Quebec border.

Decide before the redesign:

- Will you offer full French translation or just key pages?
- Do you need a language toggle or separate URL structure (/en/ and /fr/)?
- How will you handle ongoing content updates in both languages?
- Does your CMS support multilingual content natively?

If bilingual is on your roadmap, build the architecture for it now — even if you launch English-only initially. Retrofitting bilingual support into a completed site is expensive and messy.

## 6. Choose the Right Platform

Platform selection is one of the highest-impact decisions in your redesign. Ontario businesses commonly evaluate WordPress, Squarespace, Shopify, and Webflow.

Here's the reality for each:

**WordPress** still powers a large percentage of Ontario business sites, but its maintenance burden is significant. Security patches, plugin conflicts, and performance optimization require ongoing attention. If you're currently on WordPress, consider a [migration to Webflow](/services/wordpress-migration/) — many Ontario businesses are making this switch to eliminate the maintenance headaches.

**Squarespace** works for simple sites but falls apart when you need custom interactions, complex layouts, or serious SEO control. Most growing Ontario businesses outgrow it within 18 months.

**Shopify** is excellent for e-commerce (especially with Waterloo-based Shopify's deep integration into Ontario's tech ecosystem) but limited for service-based businesses.

**Webflow** gives you the design freedom of custom code with the convenience of a visual editor, plus built-in hosting that consistently delivers sub-second load times. For Ontario professional services, SaaS companies, and mid-market businesses, it's become the platform of choice.

## 7. Design for Mobile-First

With 72% of Ontario web traffic coming from mobile devices, designing desktop-first and then "making it responsive" is backwards. Your redesign should start with the mobile layout and expand upward.

Key mobile considerations for Ontario businesses:

- **Touch targets** at least 44px × 44px (especially important for click-to-call buttons)
- **Simplified navigation** — hamburger menus are fine, but make sure key pages are accessible within two taps
- **Fast-loading hero sections** — avoid massive background videos that eat through mobile data
- **Local elements** — tap-to-call, tap-to-map, and tap-to-email should be prominent
- **Form simplification** — reduce form fields for mobile; Ontario users on the GO train aren't filling out 15-field contact forms

Test your redesign on actual devices, not just browser emulators. The experience on a phone in a Rogers dead zone on the subway is very different from Chrome DevTools on a MacBook.

## 8. Create a Content Migration Plan

Content migration is where most redesigns go wrong. You need a systematic plan for every piece of content on your current site.

Create a spreadsheet with:

| Current URL | Keep/Update/Remove | New URL | 301 Redirect Needed | Content Updates Required |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| /about-us | Keep | /about | Yes | Update team bios, add Toronto office photos |
| /services/old-service | Remove | N/A | Redirect to /services | N/A |
| /blog/post-from-2019 | Update | /blog/same-slug | No | Refresh stats, update CTAs |

For Ontario businesses with location-specific content (multiple GTA offices, service areas across the province), make sure your location pages are accounted for individually. Losing a well-ranking "/services/toronto" page because it got merged into a generic services page is a painful and avoidable mistake.

## 9. Optimize for Local SEO

Ontario businesses depend heavily on local search. Your redesign must protect and enhance your local SEO foundation.

Critical local SEO elements for your new site:

- **Google Business Profile alignment** — NAP (Name, Address, Phone) must match exactly between your site and GBP
- **Location-specific pages** if you serve multiple Ontario cities (Toronto, Ottawa, Hamilton, London, Kitchener-Waterloo)
- **Local schema markup** — LocalBusiness, Service, and FAQ schema implemented properly
- **Ontario-specific keywords** naturally integrated into page titles, H1s, and meta descriptions
- **Neighbourhood-level content** where relevant (e.g., "serving businesses in Liberty Village, King West, and the Financial District")

## 10. Plan Your 301 Redirect Strategy

Every URL that changes needs a 301 redirect. Every. Single. One. This is how you preserve the search equity your current site has built up.

Steps:

1. Crawl your current site with Screaming Frog or Ahrefs to get a complete URL list
2. Map every old URL to its new equivalent
3. Identify pages with significant backlinks (use Ahrefs or Moz) and prioritize those redirects
4. Set up redirects before launch, not after
5. Test every redirect after launch

For Ontario businesses migrating from WordPress to Webflow, the URL structure often changes significantly (WordPress loves those /category/subcategory/post-name/ patterns). The [WordPress migration process](/services/wordpress-migration/) should include a comprehensive redirect map as a deliverable.

## 11. Implement Proper Analytics and Tracking

Your new site needs measurement infrastructure from day one. Don't launch and then "set up analytics later."

Pre-launch tracking checklist:

- **Google Analytics 4** properly configured with Ontario-relevant events
- **Google Search Console** verified and connected
- **Conversion tracking** on all forms, phone clicks, and key actions
- **Heatmap tools** (Hotjar or Microsoft Clarity) installed for post-launch optimization
- **Google Tag Manager** for flexible tracking without code changes
- **Custom events** for key Ontario business actions (e.g., "Request a Quote" clicks, office location views, service area page visits)

Set up comparison dashboards before launch so you can measure the redesign's impact against your baseline data from Step 1.

## 12. Build a Pre-Launch QA Checklist

Quality assurance catches the issues that cost you customers. Test systematically across:

### Functionality Testing
- All forms submit correctly and send notifications
- All links work (internal and external)
- Search functionality returns relevant results
- E-commerce checkout flow completes (if applicable)

### Cross-Browser Testing
- Chrome, Safari, Firefox, Edge (minimum)
- Test on both Mac and Windows — Ontario's business market is roughly 60/40 Windows/Mac

### Device Testing
- iPhone (Safari), Android (Chrome), iPad
- Test on actual devices, not just emulators

### Content Review
- No placeholder text ("Lorem ipsum" appearing on a live site is embarrassingly common)
- All images have alt text (AODA requirement)
- Phone numbers and addresses are correct
- Ontario-specific details (HST rates, local regulations mentioned) are accurate and current

## 13. Develop Your Launch Day Plan

Don't launch on a Friday afternoon. Ontario businesses should aim for a Tuesday or Wednesday morning launch, giving you the full work week to monitor and fix issues.

Your launch day plan should include:

- **DNS propagation window** — allow 24-48 hours for full propagation
- **Team notification** — everyone who interacts with the website needs to know
- **Client communication** — if clients use your site for portals or resources, give advance notice
- **Social media announcement** — have posts ready for LinkedIn (the dominant B2B platform in Ontario's professional market)
- **Monitoring schedule** — check analytics, Search Console, and form submissions hourly for the first day

## 14. Plan Post-Launch Optimization

Launching is not the finish line. The first 90 days after launch are critical for optimization.

Post-launch priorities for Ontario businesses:

- **Week 1:** Monitor Core Web Vitals, fix any performance issues, verify all redirects are working
- **Week 2-4:** Analyze user behaviour with heatmaps, identify drop-off points in conversion funnels
- **Month 2:** A/B test key landing pages, refine CTAs based on data
- **Month 3:** Full SEO audit to compare rankings against pre-launch baseline, adjust content strategy

The Ontario market moves fast. Your competitors aren't standing still while you optimize, so build a continuous improvement cadence into your post-launch plan.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How long does a typical website redesign take for Ontario businesses?

Most Ontario website redesigns take 12-20 weeks from kickoff to launch, depending on complexity. A simple 10-page brochure site can be done in 8-10 weeks, while a complex site with custom integrations, bilingual content, and e-commerce functionality may take 20-30 weeks. The planning and discovery phase (2-3 weeks) is critical — skipping it to "save time" almost always results in a longer overall timeline due to mid-project scope changes.

### Will a website redesign hurt my Google rankings?

It can if done incorrectly, but a properly executed redesign with comprehensive 301 redirects, maintained content quality, and preserved URL structures should protect your rankings. Many Ontario businesses actually see ranking improvements after a redesign because the new site loads faster, works better on mobile, and has cleaner technical SEO. The key is having a detailed redirect map and monitoring Search Console closely for the first 30 days after launch.

### Do Ontario businesses need AODA-compliant websites?

Yes. Under the Accessibility for Ontarians with Disabilities Act, all organizations with 50+ employees must have websites meeting WCAG 2.0 Level AA standards. However, best practice is for all Ontario businesses to meet these standards regardless of size — it improves usability for everyone and protects you from potential legal issues. Non-compliance can result in fines of up to $100,000 per day for corporations.
