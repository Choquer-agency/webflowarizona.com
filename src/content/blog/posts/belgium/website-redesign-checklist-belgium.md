Belgium might be small geographically, but its digital market is deceptively complex. Three official languages, distinct regional economies, a bilingual capital, and European Union institutions creating a massive international audience in Brussels — your website redesign has to navigate all of this. A one-size-fits-all approach doesn't work here.

Whether you're a logistics company in Antwerp's port district, a technology firm in the Ghent tech ecosystem, a consultancy serving EU institutions in the European Quarter, a pharmaceutical company in Walloon Brabant, or a diamond trader on Pelikaanstraat, your website needs to perform across Belgium's fragmented but lucrative market.

## 1. Audit Your Current Website Performance

Belgium's three linguistic communities mean your analytics tell three different stories. Examine each one.

Key metrics:

- **Traffic by region** — Break down visitors from Flanders, Wallonia, and Brussels separately. Their behaviour patterns differ.
- **Language usage** — What percentage of visitors use Dutch, French, English, or German? This informs your content investment allocation.
- **Mobile traffic** — Belgium's mobile web usage is approximately 73%, with Brussels skewing higher due to younger demographics and commuter browsing
- **Core Web Vitals** — Performance scores on every key page; Belgian internet speeds average 100+ Mbps but vary between urban and rural areas
- **Conversion rates by language** — Are your Dutch pages converting better than French pages (or vice versa)? This reveals content quality gaps.
- **Top performing content** — Identify which pages and blog posts drive the most traffic and conversions in each language

Create separate performance baselines for each language version. A redesign that improves the Dutch site but degrades the French experience (or vice versa) isn't a success.

## 2. Define Goals for Belgium's Fragmented Market

Belgium's economy is driven by logistics, pharmaceuticals, chemicals, technology, financial services, and EU-related consulting. Each sector intersects differently with the country's linguistic reality.

Strategic questions:

- Do you serve all of Belgium or specific regions? A Flemish manufacturing company might not need French content, while a Brussels consultancy absolutely does.
- Are you targeting EU institutions and the international community in Brussels? This requires strong English content.
- Is the redesign supporting expansion — from Flanders into Wallonia, or from Belgium into neighbouring Luxembourg and the Netherlands?
- Do you need to improve recruitment in a market where talent competition is fierce across tech, pharma, and finance?
- What measurable outcomes define success? Belgian stakeholders expect precision in reporting.

Belgium's unique position at the heart of Europe means many Belgian businesses serve international markets. Your redesign goals should reflect whether you're optimizing for domestic, European, or global audiences.

## 4. Meet Belgian Accessibility Requirements

Belgium follows the EU Web Accessibility Directive, transposed into Belgian law. The Belgian federal and regional governments are progressively extending accessibility requirements.

Your accessibility checklist:

- **WCAG 2.1 Level AA** across all pages in all languages
- **Accessibility statement** — Required for public sector and increasingly expected for private sector
- **Colour contrast** meeting minimum ratios
- **Keyboard navigation** for all interactive elements
- **Screen reader compatibility** — Test with NVDA (common in Belgium) and VoiceOver
- **Multilingual alt text** — Alt text must be in the correct language for each version of the site
- **Form accessibility** — Labels and error messages in Dutch, French, and English as appropriate
- **Accessible documents** — PDFs and other downloadable files must be accessible (common in Belgian business)

Belgium's accessibility enforcement is strengthening with the European Accessibility Act. Build compliance into your redesign now rather than facing costly remediation later.

## 5. Plan Trilingual (or More) Content

Belgium's linguistic landscape is your biggest redesign challenge and your biggest opportunity to outperform competitors who handle it poorly.

Language strategy:

- **Dutch (Nederlands)** — For Flanders and the Dutch-speaking community in Brussels. Use standard Dutch, not Flemish dialect, for written content. However, note some terminology differences between Belgian Dutch (Flemish) and Netherlands Dutch.
- **French (Français)** — For Wallonia, the French-speaking community in Brussels, and French-speaking international visitors. Use standard French with Belgian conventions where relevant.
- **German (Deutsch)** — For the German-speaking community in eastern Belgium (roughly 77,000 speakers around Eupen and Sankt Vith). Many national-serving businesses include German; smaller businesses may skip it.
- **English** — Essential for Brussels-based businesses serving EU institutions, international organizations, and expat communities.

Technical implementation:

- URL structure: /nl/, /fr/, /de/, /en/ with comprehensive hreflang tags
- CMS supporting parallel content management in 3-4 languages
- Professional translation for each language — machine translation is obvious and damaging to credibility
- Layout accommodations — Dutch and French text lengths differ significantly; German tends to be longest
- Language detection and redirection — based on browser language preference, with easy manual override

The quality of your multilingual implementation directly reflects your understanding of Belgium. Competitors who do it badly — broken translations, missing pages, inconsistent navigation — create an opportunity for businesses that get it right.

## 7. Design for Belgium's Mobile-First Reality

With 73% of Belgian web traffic on mobile, your redesign must prioritize the smartphone experience.

Mobile design considerations for Belgium:

- **Commuter browsing** — Many Belgians browse during train commutes (NMBS/SNCB). Design for the interrupted, attention-limited mobile session.
- **Language switching on mobile** — The language toggle must be prominent and easy to use on small screens. A Belgian user in Brussels might switch between Dutch and French regularly.
- **Touch-friendly navigation** — Minimum 44px touch targets for all interactive elements
- **Fast loading** — Optimize for variable connection quality, especially on mobile networks in rural Wallonia and Flemish countryside
- **Simplified forms** — Belgian users on mobile won't complete lengthy forms. 3-5 fields maximum.
- **Local actions** — Click-to-call, click-to-map, and click-to-email prominent for service businesses

Test on both iPhone (Safari) and Android (Chrome). Belgium's market is roughly 50/50 iPhone/Android, closer to 55/45 iPhone in Brussels.

## 8. Plan Content Migration

Multilingual content migration for Belgian sites is complex. You're effectively migrating 3-4 sites simultaneously.

Migration framework:

1. **Inventory all content in all languages** — pages, blog posts, resources, downloads
2. **Score each piece** — traffic, conversions, SEO value, brand importance
3. **Categorize:** Keep, Rewrite, Consolidate, Remove — for each language version independently
4. **Check language parity** — Are there pages that exist in Dutch but not French? Content gaps between languages should be addressed in the redesign.
5. **Map old URLs to new URLs** in all languages
6. **Update Belgian-specific content:**
   - Regulatory references (Belgian law changes)
   - Industry data and statistics
   - Regional office information
   - EU-related content if applicable

Pay particular attention to legal pages. Belgian commercial websites must include specific legal information, and this varies slightly between federal and regional requirements.

## 10. Build Redirect Maps for All Languages

Redirects for Belgian sites must cover all language versions. Missing a redirect in one language version is still a failure.

Process:

1. Crawl all language versions of your site (/nl/, /fr/, /de/, /en/)
2. Map every old URL to its new equivalent in each language
3. Handle cross-language URL patterns (e.g., /nl/diensten/ → /nl/services/ and /fr/services/ → /fr/services/)
4. Prioritize pages with backlinks from Belgian media and organizations
5. Implement all redirects before launch
6. Verify with post-launch crawls in each language

For businesses [migrating from WordPress](/services/wordpress-migration/), the URL restructuring combined with multilingual mapping creates significant complexity. Budget adequate time for this critical task.

## 11. Configure GDPR-Compliant Analytics

Belgium's Data Protection Authority (Autorité de protection des données/Gegevensbeschermingsautoriteit) actively enforces GDPR. Your analytics must be compliant.

Setup checklist:

- **Cookie consent** — Compliant banner in Dutch, French, and English (at minimum) with equal prominence for accept and refuse options
- **Google Analytics 4** — Configured with consent mode
- **Conversion tracking** — Events for all business-critical actions
- **Language-segmented reporting** — Dashboard segments for Dutch, French, and English user behaviour
- **Privacy policies** — Complete and accurate in each language
- **Data processing register** — Document all data processing activities as required by GDPR

The Belgian DPA has been involved in landmark GDPR enforcement actions. Ensure your setup meets the highest standard.

## 12. Execute Multilingual QA

QA for Belgian websites is inherently complex. Every test must be performed in every language version.

### Language QA
- Native Dutch speaker reviews all Dutch content
- Native French speaker reviews all French content
- German content reviewed by native speaker (if applicable)
- English content reviewed for quality
- No cross-language contamination (Dutch content appearing on French pages)
- Language toggle functions correctly from every page

### Cultural and Legal Accuracy
- Prices in EUR with correct Belgian formatting: € 10.000,00 (period as thousand separator, comma as decimal)
- Belgian phone numbers formatted correctly: +32 2 XXX XX XX
- Postal codes correct (4-digit Belgian format)
- Legal mentions (mentions légales/wettelijke vermeldingen) complete and accurate
- VAT number format correct (BE 0XXX.XXX.XXX)

### Technical Testing
- Cross-browser in Chrome, Safari, Firefox, Edge
- iPhone Safari and Android Chrome
- Performance from Belgian and international locations
- Forms tested in all languages including special characters

## 13. Plan Your Belgian Launch

Belgian business has its own calendar and culture. Plan your launch accordingly.

Timing:

- **Ideal:** Tuesday, Wednesday, or Thursday, 09:00-10:00 CET
- **Avoid:** Belgian holidays (there are many, including regional holidays unique to Flanders or Wallonia), August, the period between Christmas and New Year
- **Consider:** Avoid launching during major Belgian events (Tomorrowland, Gentse Feesten) if your audience overlaps

Launch checklist:

- DNS and SSL verification
- Cookie consent tested in all languages
- Legal pages published in all languages
- Team notification across offices (potentially in multiple linguistic regions)
- LinkedIn announcement (LinkedIn is dominant for B2B in Belgium)
- Monitor analytics by language region for the first 48 hours

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How many languages does a Belgian business website need?

It depends on your market. Businesses serving all of Belgium need at minimum Dutch and French. Adding English is strongly recommended if you serve international clients, EU institutions, or the Brussels expat community. German is appropriate for businesses specifically serving Belgium's German-speaking community in the east. Brussels-based businesses serving EU institutions often need Dutch, French, and English at minimum. Each language adds 30-40% to content and development costs, so be strategic about which pages need all languages versus which can be prioritized.

### What legal requirements apply to Belgian commercial websites?

Belgian law requires commercial websites to display the company name and legal form, registered office address, enterprise number (KBO/BCE), VAT identification number, and contact information. If you sell online, you need conditions générales de vente / algemene verkoopsvoorwaarden (terms of sale). A GDPR-compliant privacy policy and cookie consent mechanism are also mandatory. These legal pages must be available in the language of the visitor, meaning you need legal content in Dutch and French at minimum for a site serving all of Belgium.

### What's the biggest challenge in a Belgian website redesign?

Managing multilingual content quality across all language versions. Many Belgian businesses launch with strong Dutch and French content but allow one language to deteriorate over time. Your redesign should establish a content management workflow that ensures all language versions stay current and maintain equal quality. This includes dedicated content owners for each language, a translation workflow with native speakers, and regular content audits to catch parity gaps. The technical redesign is the easier part — sustaining multilingual content quality is the ongoing challenge.
