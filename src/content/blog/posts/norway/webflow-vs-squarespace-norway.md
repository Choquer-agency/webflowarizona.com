**For Norwegian maritime and energy companies, Webflow outperforms Squarespace in nearly every dimension that matters — design flexibility, CMS depth, GDPR compliance control, multilingual architecture, and the ability to build custom interfaces that serve complex stakeholder audiences.** Squarespace works for simple brochure sites, but the moment a Stavanger energy firm or Bergen shipping company needs regulatory documentation sections, investor portals, or multi-language content in Bokmål, Nynorsk, and English, Squarespace's template constraints become a serious liability.

Norway's maritime and energy industries account for roughly 42% of the country's total export revenue, according to Statistics Norway (SSB). The digital presence of these companies isn't a marketing afterthought — it's a critical interface between complex industrial operations and global partners, regulators, investors, and talent. Choosing the wrong platform costs Norwegian companies far more than the subscription fee suggests.

## Why Is This Comparison Different for Norwegian Industrial Companies?

Most Webflow vs Squarespace comparisons evaluate the platforms through the lens of freelancers, restaurants, and small creative agencies. That framing is almost useless for a company like Odfjell SE in Bergen, DOF Group in Austevoll, or the dozens of energy firms operating out of Forus near Stavanger. Norwegian industrial companies face platform requirements that most comparison articles never address.

### The Regulatory Documentation Problem

Maritime companies registered with the Norwegian Maritime Authority (Sjøfartsdirektoratet) and energy firms reporting to the Norwegian Petroleum Directorate (Oljedirektoratet) maintain extensive public-facing documentation. Safety records, environmental impact assessments, fleet specifications, field development plans — this content demands structured CMS architecture that can handle hundreds of interconnected documents with proper categorization, search functionality, and access controls.

Squarespace's CMS is built for blog posts and simple product pages. It offers one blog, a handful of collection types, and no ability to create custom content structures. When a maritime company needs to organize vessel specifications by class, route, and capacity — with each vessel page linking to relevant compliance documentation — Squarespace simply cannot accommodate the architecture.

Webflow's CMS supports up to 40 custom collection types with reference fields that link content across structures. A Bergen shipping company can build vessel databases, crew certification records, sustainability reports, and news feeds as interconnected but independently managed content structures. This isn't a theoretical advantage — it's the difference between a functional industrial website and a frustrating workaround.

### Multilingual Requirements That Squarespace Ignores

Norwegian businesses operate in a trilingual reality: Bokmål for eastern Norway, Nynorsk for western regions (particularly relevant for Bergen-based maritime companies), and English for international audiences. Many companies serving the Barents Sea region also need content in Russian or languages of their international partners.

Squarespace has no native multilingual support. None. Building a Norwegian/English bilingual site on Squarespace requires clunky workarounds — duplicate pages, subdirectories managed manually, or third-party apps like Weglot that add cost and complexity. For a company in Drammen or Fredrikstad serving both domestic and Scandinavian markets, this is a dealbreaker from day one.

Webflow introduced native localization that supports proper hreflang tags, locale-specific content management, and clean URL structures (/no/, /en/, /nn/) from a single project. For a Trondheim-based SINTEF spin-off publishing research in both Norwegian and English, this means one content team managing one project instead of maintaining parallel sites.

### GDPR Compliance at the Infrastructure Level

Norway, as an EEA member, enforces GDPR through the Norwegian Data Protection Authority (Datatilsynet). The penalties are real — Datatilsynet fined Grindr NOK 65 million in 2021 for GDPR violations, demonstrating that Norwegian regulators take enforcement seriously.

Squarespace's data processing infrastructure is US-based, and its cookie consent tools are rudimentary. You get a basic cookie banner with limited configuration. For a Norwegian energy company handling inquiries from EU-based partners and processing contact form data, the lack of granular consent management creates compliance risk.

Webflow allows custom cookie consent implementations, gives you control over which scripts fire before and after consent, and supports integration with enterprise-grade consent management platforms like Cookiebot (a Scandinavian company, headquartered in Copenhagen). You can architect GDPR compliance into the site rather than bolting it on as an afterthought.

## How Does Scandinavian Design Philosophy Expose Squarespace's Limits?

Norwegian design culture — rooted in the functionalist tradition of Scandinavian modernism — values restraint, precision, and purposeful whitespace. Walk through the exhibitions at Nasjonalmuseet in Oslo or study the branding of companies like Hurtigruten and you'll recognize a consistent visual language: clean typography, generous negative space, and design decisions that serve function before decoration.

Squarespace templates approximate this aesthetic. Some of their newer templates look sleek and minimal out of the box. The problem emerges when you need to go beyond the template.

### Typography Control

Norwegian companies frequently use brand typefaces that aren't available in Squarespace's font library. Squarespace supports Google Fonts and Adobe Fonts, but uploading custom WOFF2 files requires code injection workarounds. Webflow supports custom font uploads natively, with full control over font-display behavior, variable font settings, and per-element typographic adjustments.

For a company like Kongsberg Gruppen — which uses specific typography as part of its defense and maritime technology brand identity — this level of control isn't optional.

### Micro-Interactions and Motion

The current wave of Scandinavian web design embraces subtle, purposeful motion. Scroll-triggered animations, state transitions, and hover effects that feel intentional rather than decorative. Norwegian agencies like Bleed and Snøhetta's digital team produce work where motion is integral to the user experience.

Webflow's native animation engine (Interactions 2.0) provides timeline-based, scroll-based, and hover-based animations without any code or plugins. Squarespace offers a handful of pre-built animation effects — fade-in, slide-up — with no ability to customize timing, easing, or trigger points. The gap between what Squarespace can animate and what Norwegian design standards expect is enormous.

### Layout Precision

Squarespace's section-based layout system works within predefined grid patterns. You can rearrange blocks within sections, but you cannot break the grid. Webflow gives you direct access to CSS Grid and Flexbox, meaning you can create asymmetric layouts, overlapping elements, and bespoke grid structures that Squarespace's system physically cannot produce.

When Bergen-based design studios like Heydays or Anti create award-winning digital work, they're building layouts that require this level of structural control. Replicating their design language in Squarespace would require abandoning the template system entirely and writing custom CSS — at which point you've lost the only advantage Squarespace had.

## When Squarespace Actually Makes Sense in Norway

Squarespace isn't a bad platform. For certain Norwegian use cases, it's perfectly adequate.

**Simple restaurant or café sites in Grünerløkka or Aker Brygge:** If you need a menu, hours, location, and a reservation link, Squarespace delivers a polished result fast.

**Solo consultants and freelancers:** A management consultant in Oslo who needs a professional single-page site with a contact form will find Squarespace faster and cheaper than Webflow.

**Event-based businesses:** If you're running a Bergen fjord tour company and need a booking page with beautiful photography, Squarespace's templates are genuinely good for this use case.

**Personal portfolios:** Norwegian photographers, architects, and designers building portfolio sites will find Squarespace's image-forward templates compelling.

The pattern is clear: Squarespace excels when content is simple, the audience is single-language, and the design needs align with available templates. The moment a Norwegian business outgrows these constraints — and most industrial and tech companies do immediately — Squarespace becomes a limitation rather than a tool.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can Squarespace handle a bilingual Norwegian/English website?

Not natively. Squarespace has no built-in multilingual support, so you'd need workarounds like Weglot (starting at ~3,600 NOK/year) or manually duplicated pages. Webflow offers native localization with proper hreflang tags, shared design updates across locales, and clean URL structures — making it far more practical for Norwegian businesses that need Bokmål and English content.

### Is Squarespace GDPR-compliant for Norwegian businesses?

Squarespace provides a basic cookie consent banner, but its data processing is US-based and its consent management tools are limited. Norwegian businesses subject to Datatilsynet oversight should evaluate whether Squarespace's built-in tools meet their specific compliance obligations. Webflow offers more granular control over cookie consent, script loading, and data processing configurations.

### Which platform performs better for SEO on Google.no?

Webflow produces cleaner semantic HTML, gives you full control over meta tags and schema markup, and consistently scores higher on Core Web Vitals — all of which influence rankings on Google.no. Squarespace handles basic SEO adequately but offers less control over technical SEO elements like custom schema, canonical tags, and page speed optimization.

### How much does it cost to migrate a Norwegian company website from Squarespace to Webflow?

Migration costs depend on site complexity. A straightforward 15–25 page bilingual site typically costs 25,000–60,000 NOK including design improvements and content migration. Larger sites with custom CMS structures, like maritime fleet databases or multi-division energy company sites, can range from 60,000–150,000 NOK. The investment typically pays for itself within 12–18 months through reduced maintenance costs and improved conversion rates.

### Does Webflow support Norwegian payment solutions like Vipps?

Webflow's e-commerce supports Stripe natively, which handles Norwegian Kroner. Squarespace also supports Stripe but offers fewer options for custom payment integrations. For most Norwegian B2B companies in maritime and energy, the primary conversion point is a contact form rather than e-commerce — and Webflow's native forms are more capable and GDPR-configurable than Squarespace's.
