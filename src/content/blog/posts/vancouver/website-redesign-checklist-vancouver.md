**A website redesign checklist for Vancouver businesses includes 15 essential steps: audit current site performance, define goals aligned with Vancouver's competitive tech and creative markets, research local competitors, ensure BC accessibility compliance, plan for multilingual audiences (English, Mandarin, Punjabi), select a modern platform like Webflow, design mobile-first for a city that lives on smartphones, optimize for local SEO across Metro Vancouver, and execute a structured launch. Following this checklist prevents the ranking drops and budget overruns that derail most Vancouver redesign projects.**

Vancouver's digital landscape is unlike anywhere else in Canada. You've got a tech hub rivalling Toronto's, a film industry that's earned the city its "Hollywood North" nickname, a thriving sustainability sector, and a real estate market where every brokerage needs a website that actually converts. The competition for online attention in this city is intense — and a website that looked modern in 2023 already feels dated against what local competitors are launching today.

Whether you're based in Gastown, the Mount Pleasant tech corridor, or out in Burnaby or Surrey, your website redesign needs to be strategic, not cosmetic. Here's the complete checklist.

## 1. Audit Your Current Website Performance

Start with data, not opinions. Your current site is full of performance signals that should inform every decision in your redesign.

Pull 12 months of analytics and focus on:

- **Traffic by source** — Vancouver businesses often have a strong direct/branded search component; understand your mix
- **Top landing pages** — Which pages are doing the heavy lifting? These must not lose performance in the transition
- **Mobile vs. desktop split** — Vancouver skews high on mobile usage (roughly 74% of local traffic), partly thanks to excellent transit connectivity where people browse on SkyTrain
- **Local search queries** — What "[service] + Vancouver" or "[service] + BC" terms are driving traffic?
- **Page speed scores** — Run Core Web Vitals on every key page; Vancouver's tech-savvy audience has zero patience for slow sites

Create a performance baseline spreadsheet. Without this, you'll never know whether your redesign actually improved things or just looked prettier.

## 2. Define Business Goals for the Redesign

"We need a new website" isn't a goal. Define what the redesigned site must accomplish:

- Are you trying to generate more leads from the Vancouver market specifically?
- Do you need to expand your reach across BC — from Victoria to Kelowna to Prince George?
- Is the redesign tied to a rebrand or new service launch?
- Do you need better e-commerce functionality for local delivery or services?

Vancouver businesses operate in very different contexts. A cleantech startup in the Innovation District has different website goals than a North Shore adventure tourism company or a Richmond-based import/export firm. Your redesign objectives should be specific to your business model and your position within Vancouver's economy.

Audit the top five competitors ranking for your target keywords:

- How fast do their sites load?
- What's their content strategy — do they have active blogs, resource centres, case studies?
- How do they handle mobile experience?

## 4. Meet BC Accessibility Standards

British Columbia's Accessible British Columbia Act (2021) is driving increased accessibility requirements for businesses. While enforcement timelines vary by organization size, the direction is clear: accessible websites are becoming a legal baseline, not a nice-to-have.

Your redesign must address:

- **WCAG 2.1 Level AA compliance** as the target standard
- **Colour contrast** meeting minimum ratios across all text and interactive elements
- **Keyboard navigation** for all functionality
- **Screen reader compatibility** with proper semantic HTML and ARIA labels
- **Captions and transcripts** for video content (especially relevant for Vancouver's content-heavy creative and tourism industries)
- **Focus management** for dynamic content and single-page application patterns

Webflow provides excellent accessibility foundations because you control the semantic HTML structure directly. No relying on WordPress plugins that bolt accessibility onto fundamentally inaccessible themes.

## 5. Plan for Vancouver's Multilingual Market

Vancouver is one of the most linguistically diverse cities in North America. Over 50% of Metro Vancouver residents speak a language other than English at home. The top languages after English are Mandarin, Cantonese, and Punjabi.

Before your redesign, decide:

- Does your business serve communities where multilingual content would drive conversions?
- If you're in real estate, immigration services, education, or healthcare — multilingual support isn't optional, it's a competitive advantage
- Will you implement full translation, key page translation, or language-specific landing pages?
- What URL structure supports your multilingual approach (/en/, /zh/, /pa/)?

Even if you launch English-only, build the architectural foundation for multilingual content now. The cost of retrofitting language support is 3-5x higher than planning for it from the start.

## 6. Select the Right Platform

Platform choice has cascading effects on every aspect of your redesign. Here's how the options stack up for Vancouver businesses specifically:

**WordPress** remains common but problematic. Vancouver's wet climate metaphor applies: like the city's rain, WordPress maintenance never stops. Security patches, plugin conflicts, and performance optimization are a constant drain. Many Vancouver businesses are choosing to [migrate away from WordPress](/services/wordpress-migration/) during their redesigns.

**Squarespace** works for freelancers and very small businesses but lacks the flexibility Vancouver's competitive market demands. When your competitor's site loads in 1.2 seconds with custom animations and yours is a template, the difference is obvious.

**Webflow** has become the platform of choice for Vancouver's design-forward businesses. It combines visual design freedom with clean code output, built-in hosting with CDN, and CMS capabilities that don't require a developer for content updates. For a city that values both aesthetics and performance, it's the natural fit.

**Custom-built** sites (React, Next.js) make sense for complex web applications but are overkill for most business websites. They also require ongoing developer resources that many Vancouver SMBs can't justify.

## 7. Design Mobile-First for Vancouver

Vancouver has one of the highest smartphone penetration rates in Canada. People browse on the SkyTrain, in coffee shops on Commercial Drive, and while waiting for the SeaBus. Your site must work flawlessly on mobile.

Mobile-first design priorities:

- **Thumb-friendly navigation** — key actions reachable without hand repositioning
- **Fast image loading** — compress aggressively; Vancouver's cell coverage is good but not perfect (looking at you, SkyTrain tunnels)
- **Click-to-call and click-to-map** prominent for service businesses
- **Simplified forms** — 3-5 fields maximum on mobile
- **Readable typography** — minimum 16px body text, generous line height
- **No hover-dependent interactions** — tooltips and hover menus fail on touch devices

Test on real devices in real conditions. The experience on an iPhone in a Kitsilano café with decent WiFi is different from an Android on cellular in the suburbs.

## 8. Map Your Content Migration

Content migration is the unglamorous work that makes or breaks a redesign. Every page, blog post, image, and downloadable resource needs a plan.

Build a migration matrix:

- **Keep as-is:** High-performing pages that just need a design refresh
- **Rewrite:** Dated content that needs updated stats, references, and CTAs
- **Consolidate:** Multiple thin pages that should merge into one comprehensive resource
- **Remove:** Outdated content with no traffic or conversion value (redirect these URLs)
- **Create new:** Content gaps your audit revealed — especially location-specific pages for different Metro Vancouver areas (Vancouver, Burnaby, Richmond, North Vancouver, Surrey)

For businesses with blogs, decide which posts are worth migrating. A post about "Vancouver web design trends for 2021" isn't worth carrying forward — redirect it to a fresh 2026 version instead.

## 9. Optimize for Local SEO Across Metro Vancouver

Local search is where Vancouver businesses live or die online. Your redesign must strengthen, not weaken, your local SEO foundation.

Essential local SEO elements:

- **Google Business Profile consistency** — your website's NAP must match your GBP exactly
- **Service area pages** — if you serve multiple municipalities within Metro Vancouver, create dedicated pages for each (Vancouver, Burnaby, Surrey, Richmond, North Vancouver, Coquitlam)
- **Local schema markup** — implement LocalBusiness, Service, and Review schema
- **Vancouver-specific keywords** — integrate naturally into page titles, headings, and content (e.g., "web design in Mount Pleasant" or "Gastown digital agency")
- **Local backlink preservation** — identify backlinks from Vancouver-based organizations (BIAs, chambers of commerce, local media) and ensure redirects protect them

## 10. Build Your 301 Redirect Map

URL changes without redirects are the single most damaging technical mistake in a website redesign. Every changed URL needs a 301 redirect.

Process:

1. **Crawl your current site** completely — use Screaming Frog, Ahrefs, or Sitebulb
2. **Export all URLs** including pages, blog posts, images, and PDFs
3. **Map old URLs to new URLs** in a spreadsheet
4. **Prioritize by value** — pages with strong backlinks or high traffic get extra attention
5. **Implement redirects before launch** — not after you notice traffic dropping
6. **Test every redirect** post-launch with a crawl tool

For Vancouver businesses [migrating from WordPress to Webflow](/services/wordpress-migration/), URL structures often change significantly. WordPress defaults like `/category/subcategory/post-name/` should map cleanly to Webflow's cleaner URL patterns. This redirect map is a critical deliverable in any platform migration.

## 11. Set Up Analytics and Conversion Tracking

Launch without analytics is flying blind. Your measurement infrastructure should be ready before the new site goes live.

Pre-launch tracking setup:

- **Google Analytics 4** with custom events for Vancouver-relevant actions
- **Google Search Console** verified and monitoring
- **Conversion goals** defined for every business-critical action (form submissions, phone calls, bookings)
- **Heatmap tracking** via Hotjar or Microsoft Clarity
- **Google Tag Manager** for flexible, no-code tracking updates
- **E-commerce tracking** if applicable (enhanced conversions for Shopify integrations)

Build a real-time monitoring dashboard that you'll check daily for the first two weeks post-launch. Set up alerts for significant traffic drops — if something breaks, you want to know in hours, not weeks.

## 12. Execute Thorough QA Testing

Testing catches the issues that embarrass you in front of clients. Be systematic.

### Cross-Browser Testing
- Chrome, Safari, Firefox, Edge — test on both Mac and Windows
- Safari is critical in Vancouver (high Mac/iPhone market share in the tech community)

### Device Testing
- iPhone 14/15/16 (Safari)
- Samsung Galaxy (Chrome)
- iPad (portrait and landscape)
- Test on actual devices, not just emulators

### Functionality Testing
- All forms submit and trigger notifications correctly
- All links (internal and external) work
- Interactive elements function properly (sliders, accordions, tabs)
- Search returns relevant results

### Content Review
- Zero placeholder text or images
- All contact information accurate and current
- Pricing reflects current rates in CAD
- Vancouver-specific references are accurate (neighbourhood names, transit references, local landmarks)

## 13. Create a Launch Day Strategy

Timing matters. For Vancouver businesses, launch on a Tuesday or Wednesday morning (Pacific Time) to give you maximum working hours for monitoring.

Launch day checklist:

- **DNS update** — point your domain to the new hosting; allow 24-48 hours for full propagation
- **SSL certificate** — verify HTTPS is working on every page
- **CDN configuration** — confirm your content delivery network is caching properly
- **Team notification** — brief your entire organization
- **Client communication** — if clients interact with your site, give advance notice
- **Social media posts** — have LinkedIn and Instagram announcements ready (both platforms are heavily used by Vancouver's business community)
- **Monitor hourly** — check analytics, Search Console, form submissions, and site performance every hour for the first 8 hours

## 14. Plan 90-Day Post-Launch Optimization

Your website redesign isn't done at launch — it's just beginning its optimization phase.

Post-launch roadmap for Vancouver businesses:

- **Days 1-7:** Fix any issues identified through monitoring, verify all redirects work, submit updated sitemap to Google
- **Days 8-30:** Analyze heatmap data, identify conversion bottlenecks, optimize top landing pages
- **Days 31-60:** Run A/B tests on key conversion elements (CTAs, form layouts, hero sections), publish fresh content to signal activity to search engines
- **Days 61-90:** Comprehensive SEO performance review comparing to pre-launch baseline, adjust strategy based on data

Vancouver's market doesn't wait. While you're optimizing, competitors are launching their own improvements. Build continuous website improvement into your operational rhythm.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I prevent losing search rankings during a Vancouver website redesign?

The key is a comprehensive 301 redirect strategy. Before launch, crawl your entire current site, map every URL to its new equivalent, and implement redirects before going live. Monitor Google Search Console daily for the first two weeks after launch to catch any crawl errors immediately. Most Vancouver businesses that follow this process see minimal ranking disruption, with rankings stabilizing or improving within 4-6 weeks.

### Should Vancouver businesses redesign on WordPress or switch to Webflow?

For most Vancouver businesses, a redesign is the ideal time to [migrate from WordPress to Webflow](/services/wordpress-migration/). WordPress's ongoing maintenance requirements — security patches, plugin updates, hosting management — are a constant drain on resources. Webflow eliminates these headaches while delivering faster page speeds, better design flexibility, and a cleaner editing experience. The Vancouver tech community has been moving toward Webflow steadily over the past two years.

### What accessibility requirements apply to Vancouver business websites?

BC's Accessible British Columbia Act is establishing accessibility standards that will apply to businesses progressively. The best practice is to meet WCAG 2.1 Level AA standards, which covers colour contrast, keyboard navigation, screen reader compatibility, and proper semantic HTML structure. Building accessibility into your redesign from the start costs a fraction of retrofitting it later, and it improves usability for all visitors.

### How much should a Vancouver business budget for a website redesign?

Budget depends on complexity. A simple 5-10 page site runs CAD $5,000-$15,000, while a mid-market 15-30 page site with CMS, blog, and lead generation features typically costs CAD $15,000-$40,000. Complex enterprise sites with custom integrations can exceed CAD $100,000. Use our [website cost calculator](/tools/website-cost-calculator/) for a more specific estimate. The critical insight: the cheapest redesign is rarely the most cost-effective when you factor in lost leads from a poorly executing site.

### How long does a typical website redesign take in Vancouver?

Plan for 12-22 weeks total. This includes 2-3 weeks for discovery, 3-6 weeks for design, 4-8 weeks for development, and 2-4 weeks for content migration and QA. Rushing a redesign to meet an arbitrary deadline almost always results in compromises that hurt performance, SEO, or user experience. If you need to launch by a specific date, work backward from that date and start planning accordingly.
