France is a market where aesthetics and regulation intersect. French businesses face some of Europe's most demanding accessibility and privacy regulations, while French consumers expect design quality that reflects the country's reputation for art, fashion, and sophistication. Your website must satisfy both the legal framework and the cultural expectation — and do it while performing well enough to compete in a market of 67 million digitally active consumers.

Whether you're a luxury brand in the 8th arrondissement, a tech startup at Station F, an industrial manufacturer in Lyon, a wine exporter in Bordeaux, or a tourism operator on the Côte d'Azur, this checklist covers every step of a properly executed French website redesign.

## 1. Audit Your Current Website Performance

Before redesigning, understand your current position. French web analytics often reveal patterns specific to the market.

Key data to collect:

- **Traffic sources** — French businesses typically see strong organic search, significant social referral from LinkedIn and Instagram, and growing referral from French media sites
- **Mobile breakdown** — France's mobile web usage sits around 76%, heavily iPhone in Paris and major cities
- **Geographic distribution** — Paris dominates, but Lyon, Marseille, Toulouse, Bordeaux, and Nantes are significant markets
- **French vs. English traffic** — What percentage of your visitors use French-language searches vs. English?
- **Core Web Vitals** — Performance scores on key pages; French internet speeds are solid (average 150+ Mbps) but vary between urban and rural areas
- **Conversion metrics** — Forms, phone calls, appointment bookings, e-commerce transactions

Build a comprehensive baseline. French stakeholders — accustomed to rigorous analysis — will expect you to demonstrate the redesign's measurable impact.

## 2. Define Goals for the French Market

France's economy spans luxury, aerospace, automotive, pharmaceutical, technology, agriculture, and tourism. Each sector has different digital priorities.

Goal-setting questions:

- Are you targeting French domestic customers, European markets, or global audiences?
- Do you need to improve your position in specific French cities or régions?
- Is the redesign supporting a digital transformation initiative? (French government incentives through France Relance and France 2030 are pushing digital adoption)
- Do you need improved recruitment features? (France's tech talent competition is fierce, especially in Paris, Lyon, and Toulouse)
- What specific metrics will define success — lead volume, conversion rate, search ranking improvements?

French businesses often have complex stakeholder environments. Get alignment on goals early — discovering mid-project that the marketing team and the direction générale have different visions is expensive.

## 4. Ensure RGAA Accessibility Compliance

France's Référentiel Général d'Amélioration de l'Accessibilité (RGAA) is one of Europe's most detailed accessibility frameworks. Based on WCAG 2.1, RGAA applies to:

- All public sector websites (mandatory since 2012)
- Companies with annual revenue exceeding €250 million (since 2023 expansion)
- Progressively extending to more private sector organizations under the European Accessibility Act

Non-compliance penalties can reach €20,000 per service per year, with mandatory publication of compliance status.

Your RGAA compliance checklist:

- **WCAG 2.1 Level AA** across all pages
- **Déclaration d'accessibilité** (accessibility statement) — mandatory, must follow the prescribed format
- **Colour contrast** meeting RGAA criteria
- **Keyboard navigation** for all interactive elements
- **Screen reader compatibility** — tested with NVDA (common in France) and VoiceOver
- **Alt text** in French for all meaningful images
- **Form accessibility** — labels, error messages, and instructions in French
- **Accessible documents** — PDFs, which are heavily used in French business, must be accessible
- **Schéma pluriannuel d'accessibilité** — larger organizations must publish a multi-year accessibility improvement plan

Webflow's clean semantic HTML output provides a strong foundation for RGAA compliance — far stronger than WordPress themes that often generate non-semantic, inaccessible markup.

## 5. Plan French-Language Content Strategy

French content requires more than translation. French audiences expect content that feels natively French — not translated from English.

Content considerations:

- **Tone and register** — French business communication is more formal than English. The vouvoiement (vous vs. tu) distinction matters even in web copy. Most B2B sites should use "vous."
- **Content length** — French web content tends to be more detailed and formal than its English equivalent. Don't assume your English content can simply be translated; it may need expansion.
- **Legal language** — French regulations require specific legal mentions (mentions légales) on every commercial website, including company registration (SIRET/SIREN), VAT number, and hosting provider details.
- **SEO in French** — French keyword research must be conducted natively, not by translating English keywords. Search behaviour differs significantly.
- **English version** — If you serve international clients, maintain an English version, but ensure French is clearly the primary language for domestic audiences.

URL structure: /fr/ and /en/ subdirectories with proper hreflang tags. French should be the default (root domain or /fr/).

## 6. Comply with RGPD (CNIL Requirements)

France's CNIL (Commission Nationale de l'Informatique et des Libertés) is one of Europe's most active data protection authorities. RGPD (GDPR in French) compliance is enforced aggressively, with CNIL issuing some of the largest GDPR fines in Europe.

Your redesign must include:

- **Cookie consent banner** — CNIL's guidelines are specific: consent must be freely given, users must be able to refuse as easily as accept, and continued browsing does NOT constitute consent
- **Politique de confidentialité** (privacy policy) — comprehensive, in French, covering all data processing
- **Mentions légales** — legally required page with company identification, hosting details, and data controller information
- **Analytics consent** — Google Analytics requires explicit consent; CNIL has specifically addressed this. Consider CNIL-exempt analytics alternatives (e.g., Matomo configured per CNIL guidelines)
- **Third-party tool disclosure** — Every external service (chat, CRM, tracking) that processes visitor data must be disclosed
- **Cookie configuration** — Technical cookies can be used without consent; marketing, analytics, and advertising cookies require opt-in

CNIL fines have reached €150 million for major companies. While SMBs face smaller penalties, the enforcement trend is clear. Build compliance into the redesign from the start.

## 7. Design for French Aesthetic Standards

France's design culture — shaped by fashion, architecture, art, and graphic design — creates specific expectations for web design.

Design principles for French business websites:

- **Elegance over flashiness** — Sophisticated colour palettes, refined typography, and purposeful whitespace
- **Typography matters deeply** — French text requires careful attention to typographic details: espaces insécables before punctuation (;, :, !, ?), guillemets (« ») for quotations, and proper handling of accented characters (é, è, ê, ë, à, ç, ù, etc.)
- **Imagery quality** — Stock photos of American business people feel foreign. Use imagery that reflects French business culture and environments.
- **Mobile-first** — 76% of French traffic is mobile; design for smartphones first
- **Video and animation** — French audiences respond well to motion design, but it must be polished and purposeful, with accessibility controls for motion
- **Brand consistency** — French businesses typically invest heavily in brand identity; your website must align perfectly with your charte graphique

## 8. Create a Structured Content Migration Plan

Content migration for French websites involves legal compliance elements that don't exist in many other markets.

Migration checklist:

- **Inventory all content** — pages, blog articles, case studies, legal documents, PDF downloads
- **Categorize:** Keep, rewrite, consolidate, or archive
- **Map old URLs to new URLs** — critical for preserving SEO equity
- **Update legal content:**
  - Mentions légales (company registration, hosting provider)
  - Conditions générales de vente (CGV) if e-commerce
  - Politique de confidentialité
  - Politique de cookies
- **Verify regulatory references** — French law changes frequently; ensure all cited regulations are current
- **Review case studies** — Client permissions, data accuracy, relevance

French businesses often have extensive PDF libraries (brochures, white papers, catalogues). Each must be migrated, updated, and made accessible per RGAA requirements.

## 10. Implement 301 Redirects

Every URL change requires a 301 redirect. For bilingual French sites, both language versions need mapping.

Process:

1. Crawl your complete current site
2. Map every URL to its new equivalent
3. Pay special attention to French-specific URL patterns (accented characters in URLs, which should be handled carefully)
4. Prioritize pages with backlinks from French media and professional directories
5. Implement before launch
6. Verify post-launch with crawl tools

For [WordPress to Webflow migrations](/services/wordpress-migration/), French WordPress sites often have complex URL structures with category hierarchies. These need careful mapping to Webflow's cleaner URL patterns.

## 11. Set Up CNIL-Compliant Analytics

Analytics for French websites must meet CNIL's specific requirements, which are among the strictest interpretations of GDPR in Europe.

Analytics setup:

- **Consent management platform** — Compliant with CNIL's latest recommendations, supporting French-language consent dialogs
- **Google Analytics 4** — Only with proper consent; CNIL has specifically ruled on analytics consent requirements
- **CNIL-exempt analytics option** — Matomo, configured per CNIL's exemption criteria, can run without cookie consent. This gives you data even when users decline analytics cookies.
- **Conversion tracking** — Events for forms, phone clicks, and business-critical actions
- **Google Search Console** — Verified for all domain configurations
- **Performance monitoring** — Real User Monitoring to track actual French user experience

Many French businesses are moving to hybrid analytics setups: CNIL-exempt Matomo for baseline data plus consent-gated GA4 for deeper analysis. This approach maximizes data availability while maintaining compliance.

## 12. Quality Assurance for French Websites

QA for French sites must cover legal, linguistic, and technical requirements.

### Legal Compliance Review
- Mentions légales complete and accurate
- Cookie consent functions correctly
- Privacy policy comprehensive and current
- CGV (conditions générales) accessible (if applicable)

### Linguistic QA
- Native French speaker review of all content
- Proper typographic conventions (espaces insécables, guillemets, accents)
- Consistent tone (vous throughout for B2B)
- No anglicisms where proper French terms exist (CNIL and the Académie Française take French language use seriously in official contexts)

### Technical Testing
- Cross-browser: Chrome, Safari, Firefox, Edge
- Devices: iPhone Safari (dominant in urban France), Android Chrome
- Performance from French servers and international locations
- French character handling in forms, URLs, and metadata
- Currency formatting: 10 000 € or 10 000,00 € (comma as decimal, space as thousand separator, € symbol after the number)

## 13. Plan Your Launch

French business operates at its own rhythm. Plan your launch accordingly.

Timing:

- **Ideal:** Tuesday, Wednesday, or Thursday, 09:00-10:00 CET
- **Avoid:** French holidays (many), August (when much of France is on vacances), the rentrée first week of September (too chaotic), pont days (bridge days around public holidays)
- **Best periods:** October-November, January-March, May (before summer wind-down)

Launch day:

- DNS propagation monitoring
- SSL verification
- Cookie consent compliance verification
- Mentions légales and privacy policy published
- Team and stakeholder notification
- LinkedIn announcement (LinkedIn France is the primary B2B platform)
- Monitoring of analytics, forms, and performance

## 14. Post-Launch Optimization

90-day plan:

French audiences are brand-loyal when they have positive experiences. Invest in post-launch optimization to build that positive impression and convert first-time visitors into long-term clients.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What are the legal requirements for French business websites?

French law requires every commercial website to include mentions légales (legal notice) with company identification (SIRET/SIREN number, RCS registration), the name of the hosting provider, the name of the publication director (directeur de publication), and VAT identification number. Additionally, you need a RGPD-compliant privacy policy, a cookie consent mechanism meeting CNIL standards, and if you sell online, conditions générales de vente (CGV). Non-compliance can result in fines and legal action. Your redesign must ensure all these elements are present, current, and properly formatted.

### How does RGAA accessibility compliance affect French website redesigns?

RGAA (Référentiel Général d'Amélioration de l'Accessibilité) requires WCAG 2.1 Level AA compliance for public sector websites and large private companies (revenue exceeding €250 million). A déclaration d'accessibilité must be published on the site. Non-compliance penalties can reach €20,000 per service per year. Even if your organization isn't currently required to comply, the European Accessibility Act is expanding requirements progressively. Building RGAA compliance into your redesign now is significantly cheaper than remediation later.

### What's the biggest compliance risk in a French website redesign?

CNIL enforcement of cookie consent and data privacy. CNIL is one of Europe's most active data protection authorities, having issued fines up to €150 million. The most common violations in website redesigns are pre-checked cookie consent boxes, collecting analytics data without consent, unclear or incomplete privacy policies, and failing to provide equal visibility to "refuse all" and "accept all" options. Build CNIL-compliant consent management into your redesign from the start, and consider CNIL-exempt analytics tools like properly configured Matomo for baseline data collection.
