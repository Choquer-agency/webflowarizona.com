**A website redesign checklist for Texas businesses covers 15 essential steps from performance auditing to post-launch optimization, addressing the unique demands of Austin's tech scene, Houston's energy sector, Dallas-Fort Worth's corporate market, and San Antonio's bilingual audience. Following this structured process ensures your redesign captures growth in the second-largest US state economy without sacrificing the organic traffic and conversion performance your business depends on.**

Texas does not do small. The state's $2.4 trillion GDP would make it the eighth-largest economy in the world if it were a country. That scale creates extraordinary business opportunity — and extraordinary competition. A web design agency in Austin competes for the same talent pool as Apple, Tesla, and Oracle. A Houston energy services company needs a digital presence that matches the sophistication of BP, ExxonMobil, and Halliburton. A Dallas law firm's website must stand up against firms with national marketing budgets.

In this environment, a website redesign is not a cosmetic exercise. It is a strategic investment that determines whether your business captures its share of Texas's explosive growth or gets left behind. This checklist gives you the structure to execute that investment with precision.

## 1. Audit Your Current Site with Texas-Scale Thinking

Texas businesses often serve multiple metro areas across enormous geographic distances. Your audit must account for this scale.

**Texas-specific audit considerations:**

- **Multi-metro performance:** Test your site speed from Dallas, Houston, Austin, San Antonio, and El Paso. A site hosted on a single East Coast server may load significantly slower for West Texas users than for Dallas users.
- **Bilingual content effectiveness:** Texas's Hispanic population exceeds 40%. If you have Spanish-language content, audit its performance independently — traffic, engagement, and conversion rates for Spanish pages versus English pages.
- **Mobile dominance:** Texas leads the nation in mobile browsing, particularly in rural areas where mobile is the primary internet connection. If your mobile experience is an afterthought, you are losing the majority of your potential audience.
- **Heat resilience:** Like Arizona, Texas summers drive users indoors to mobile devices. Analyze your June-through-September traffic patterns for shifts in device usage and session behavior.

Pull 12 months of data from Google Analytics, Search Console, and your CRM to establish baselines. Segment by metro area if your business serves multiple Texas cities.

## 2. Set Goals That Match Texas's Growth Trajectory

Texas businesses are growing fast, and redesign goals should reflect that momentum.

**Goal frameworks by Texas market:**

- **Austin tech companies (Domain, East Austin, Cedar Park):** Product signup rates, developer documentation engagement, demo conversion, and recruitment page performance. Austin's tech market is intensely competitive — your site needs to convert and recruit simultaneously.
- **Houston energy and professional services (Galleria, Energy Corridor, Midtown):** RFQ submissions, case study engagement, white paper downloads, and LinkedIn-driven referral conversion.
- **Dallas-Fort Worth corporate market (Uptown, Legacy West, Las Colinas):** Enterprise lead generation, event registration, thought leadership content engagement, and partner portal usage.
- **San Antonio and the border region:** Bilingual conversion parity (Spanish leads converting at the same rate as English), military-related service inquiries (given the five major military bases), and healthcare portal engagement.

Each goal needs a baseline, a target, and a measurement timeline. "Get more traffic" is not a Texas-sized goal. "Increase qualified lead form submissions from Houston Energy Corridor prospects by 35% within 90 days" is.

## 3. Research Competitors Across Texas's Diverse Markets

Texas's sheer geographic size means your competitive landscape varies dramatically from city to city.

**Competitive research approach:**

- **Map metro-specific competitors.** Your Austin competitors are completely different from your Houston competitors. Research each market independently.
- **Evaluate California transplant competitors.** The wave of California companies relocating to Texas — Tesla to Austin, Hewlett Packard Enterprise to Houston, CBRE to Dallas — often brings Silicon Valley web standards. Your redesign needs to meet or exceed those standards.
- **Check industry vertical competitors.** In Houston's energy sector, companies like Baker Hughes and Schlumberger set digital standards. In Dallas's financial services market, firms like Charles Schwab (now DFW-headquartered) define expectations.
- **Assess tech stacks.** Many Texas businesses, especially outside Austin, still run on dated WordPress or Squarespace sites. A modern, fast redesign on Webflow gives you an immediate competitive advantage.

## 4. Build a Content Migration Strategy for Scale

Texas businesses often have extensive content libraries covering multiple service areas, locations, and audience segments.

**Content migration plan:**

1. **Inventory everything** — pages, blog posts, case studies, location pages, PDFs, and media files. Multi-location Texas businesses (common in healthcare, legal, and home services) may have hundreds of location-specific pages.
2. **Score content by performance** — traffic, conversions, backlinks, and relevance. Keep what works, update what is close, and retire what does not perform.
3. **Plan redirects meticulously.** Every changed URL needs a 301 redirect. Texas businesses with franchise or multi-location models often have complex URL structures that require careful redirect planning.
4. **Address bilingual content migration.** If you have Spanish pages, ensure they migrate with the same care as English pages — same redirect attention, same SEO preservation.

## 6. Design Mobile-First for Texas's Sprawling Geography

Texas's geographic scale and car-centric culture mean your audience is often browsing on mobile — in parking lots, waiting rooms, and during commutes (passengers, not drivers).

**Mobile design for Texas:**

- **Fast loading on variable connections.** Drive 30 minutes outside any Texas metro and connectivity degrades significantly. Your site needs to function on 3G in Dripping Springs, Katy, and Frisco — not just 5G in downtown Austin.
- **Thumb-friendly tap targets** sized at 48x48 pixels minimum. Place CTAs in the natural thumb zone.
- **Click-to-call prominently displayed.** Texans still prefer phone calls for many business interactions, especially in professional services, home services, and healthcare.
- **Outdoor readability.** High-contrast design that remains legible in Texas sunlight — which is intense 8+ months per year.
- **Map and directions integration.** Texas businesses serve enormous geographic areas. Make it easy for mobile users to get directions to your nearest location.

## 7. Protect SEO Investment During Transition

Texas businesses competing for local search terms across multiple metros have complex SEO profiles that require careful preservation.

**SEO protection:**

- **301 redirects for every changed URL.** This is non-negotiable. Build the redirect map before development begins.
- **Preserve multi-location SEO.** If you rank for "Houston web design" and "Dallas web design" on different pages, ensure both pages and their optimization survive the redesign.
- **Maintain schema markup** and add new structured data — LocalBusiness schema for each location, FAQ schema for service pages, and Review schema where applicable.
- **Update XML sitemaps** and submit to Search Console within one hour of launch.
- **Monitor multi-metro rankings** independently. A redesign might maintain your Austin rankings while inadvertently dropping your Houston rankings if location pages are structured differently.

Texas's competitive search markets — especially for legal, medical, and home services — mean that even a 2-week ranking dip can cost significant revenue. Plan for zero tolerance on SEO disruption.

## 8. Build Accessibility and Compliance In

Texas businesses must address both ADA accessibility and the Texas Data Privacy and Security Act (TDPSA).

**Compliance requirements:**

- **WCAG 2.1 AA compliance** across all pages and interactive elements.
- **TDPSA compliance** — Texas's data privacy law took effect July 2024. Your redesign must include proper consent mechanisms, privacy disclosures, and data subject rights functionality.
- **Bilingual accessibility** — if you serve Spanish-speaking audiences, your accessibility features must work in both languages. Screen readers, alt text, and form labels all need Spanish equivalents.
- **Color contrast** at 4.5:1 minimum for normal text — critical for outdoor browsing in Texas sun.
- **Keyboard navigation** for all interactive elements.
- **Video captions** for multimedia content.

ADA web accessibility lawsuits in Texas have increased steadily. Proactive compliance during your redesign is significantly cheaper than reactive remediation.

## 9. Inventory and Test All Integrations

Texas businesses typically run robust tech stacks with multiple integration points.

**Integration audit:**

- **CRM systems:** HubSpot (very popular with Austin startups), Salesforce (dominant in Dallas enterprise), or industry-specific CRMs.
- **Booking and scheduling:** Calendly, Acuity, or industry tools — DrChrono for medical, Clio for legal, ServiceTitan for home services.
- **Payment processing:** Stripe, Square, or specialized processors.
- **Marketing platforms:** Mailchimp, Klaviyo, ActiveCampaign, plus Meta Pixel, Google Ads tracking, and TikTok Pixel.
- **Analytics:** GA4, Google Tag Manager, and any attribution platforms.
- **Location-specific tools:** Store locators, franchise management platforms, and territory-based lead routing — critical for multi-location Texas businesses.

Test every integration in staging. Submit test forms, run test transactions, and verify data flows end to end.

## 11. Execute Rigorous Pre-Launch QA

QA must account for Texas's geographic diversity and multi-metro audience.

**QA protocol:**

- **Cross-device testing** across iPhone, Samsung (popular in Texas), iPad, and desktop.
- **Multi-location speed testing** from Dallas, Houston, Austin, San Antonio, and at least one rural Texas location.
- **Form and conversion testing** — submit every form, test every booking widget, and verify all CRM data flows.
- **Bilingual content review** — if you have Spanish content, have a native speaker review all copy for accuracy and natural language.
- **Load testing** for traffic spikes — Texas businesses with seasonal peaks (hurricane season for roofing companies, tax season for accounting firms, football season for sports-related businesses) need sites that handle surges.
- **Accessibility scan** using axe DevTools and manual keyboard navigation testing.

## 12. Plan a Clean Launch

Structure your launch to minimize risk in Texas's always-on business environment.

**Launch protocol:**

1. **Schedule during low traffic** — Tuesday through Thursday, 5-7 AM Central Time.
2. **Deploy redirects with the new site.**
3. **Monitor analytics and conversion events** for the first four hours.
4. **Submit updated sitemap** to Google Search Console.
5. **Verify all integrations** including CRM, booking, and payment systems.
6. **Notify your team across all locations** — Texas businesses with multiple offices need coordinated internal communication.

Avoid launching during SXSW (Austin), the Houston Livestock Show and Rodeo, State Fair of Texas (Dallas), or any major event that affects your traffic patterns.

## 13. Monitor and Iterate Post-Launch

Post-launch monitoring should reflect Texas's multi-market reality.

**Monitoring cadence:**

- **Days 1-7:** Daily checks on traffic, conversions, and crawl errors — segmented by metro area.
- **Weeks 2-4:** Weekly comparisons against pre-redesign baselines for each Texas market you serve.
- **Months 2-3:** A/B testing on key conversion pages. Test different approaches for different Texas metros if your analytics support segmentation.
- **Month 3:** Comprehensive review against original KPIs.

Texas's business environment is relentlessly competitive. Companies that treat their website as a continuously improving product outperform those that redesign once and neglect it until the next overhaul.

### Should my Texas business website be bilingual?

If your business serves customers in San Antonio, El Paso, the Rio Grande Valley, or any Texas market with a significant Hispanic population, a bilingual website is a competitive advantage. At minimum, consider Spanish versions of your key conversion pages (homepage, service pages, contact page). Full bilingual sites with proper hreflang implementation give you access to search queries in both languages, effectively doubling your potential organic traffic in bilingual markets.

### How do I redesign without losing my Google rankings in Texas?

The three essential elements are: comprehensive 301 redirects for every changed URL, preserved on-page SEO elements (titles, metas, headers, content), and immediate sitemap submission after launch. For multi-location Texas businesses, pay special attention to location page SEO — ensure each city page maintains its optimized content and internal linking. Monitor Search Console daily for the first two weeks and fix any crawl errors immediately.

### When is the best time to launch a redesigned website in Texas?

Avoid launching during your peak business season or major Texas events that affect your traffic. For most Texas businesses, the best launch windows are January (post-holiday), April-May (between spring events and summer), or September (post-summer, pre-holiday). Launch mid-week (Tuesday-Thursday) during low-traffic hours (5-7 AM Central) for the smoothest transition.
