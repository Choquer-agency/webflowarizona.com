## How to Choose a Web Agency When You're in Montana

### The Local vs. Remote Decision

This is the central tension for Montana businesses choosing a web agency. Local agencies offer face-to-face relationships and local market understanding. Remote agencies — including specialists who serve Montana from elsewhere — offer deeper platform expertise and often better pricing.

Here's a practical framework:

**Choose local if:** Your project is straightforward (5-10 page business site), you strongly value in-person meetings, and you want someone you can grab lunch with when issues arise.

**Choose a specialist (local or remote) if:** You need high performance scores, complex CMS architecture, migration from another platform, or a site that needs to compete with out-of-state competitors. The quality gap between a Webflow generalist and a certified specialist is significant, and it shows up in your search rankings and conversion rates.

For most Montana businesses, the pragmatic answer is the specialist. Modern project management tools, video calls, and screen sharing make remote collaboration seamless. And a certified Webflow expert who has completed 150+ projects will deliver a fundamentally better site than a local generalist building their 10th Webflow project.

### What Montana Businesses Should Specifically Demand

1. **Mobile-first performance.** Over 65% of Montana web searches happen on mobile, and many are on variable cellular connections. Demand that your agency deliver a Lighthouse mobile score above 90. If they can't commit to a specific number, that's a red flag.

2. **Local SEO infrastructure.** Montana businesses live and die by local search. Your agency should set up proper Google Business Profile integration, local schema markup, and location-specific content strategy. Ask specifically how they approach local SEO for Montana businesses.

3. **CMS training that sticks.** The whole point of Webflow is that your team can manage content after launch. Your agency should provide video training, written documentation, and a reasonable window of post-launch support for questions. Don't accept a site handoff where you're left staring at an unfamiliar interface.

4. **Hosting and maintenance clarity.** Webflow includes hosting in its platform fee, which eliminates the separate hosting bills and security update cycles that WordPress requires. Make sure your agency explains the ongoing costs clearly — Webflow's site plans range from $14-$39/month for most business sites.

5. **Image handling for Montana's visual market.** Whether you're selling ranch experiences, outdoor gear, or scenic property, your site will be image-heavy. Demand that your agency implements proper image optimization — responsive images, lazy loading, next-gen formats — so your beautiful Montana photography doesn't slow your site to a crawl.

### Red Flags Specific to Montana's Market

- **"We can build it on WordPress for less."** They probably can. It'll also be slower, harder to maintain, and more vulnerable. The long-term cost of WordPress in Montana — where finding a developer for emergency fixes is harder — makes Webflow the more practical investment.
- **No portfolio of live sites.** Designs in PDFs and Figma files don't prove anything about performance. Insist on seeing live, functioning websites you can test yourself.
- **Vague timelines.** A Webflow site for a Montana small business should take 3-6 weeks, not 3-6 months. Extended timelines usually mean the agency is juggling too many projects or lacks Webflow efficiency.
- **No mention of mobile.** If an agency presents desktop-only mockups without discussing mobile strategy, they're behind the curve. More Montanans access the web on phones than desktops.
